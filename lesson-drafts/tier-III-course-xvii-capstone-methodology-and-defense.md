# The Capstone: Methodology, Topic, and the Oral Defense

*On the discipline of saying something one is prepared, in the presence of others, to defend.*

Sixteen courses ago, you were asked to suspend a question. Now you are asked to answer one — in writing, at length, and aloud, before three of us who have read what you wrote and intend to press on the soft places. The capstone is not a final examination of what you have learned. It is the first occasion on which the school treats you as a colleague rather than a student: someone who has earned the right to a thesis, and the corresponding obligation to defend it without recourse to the authority of those who taught you.

## I. From Topic to Problem

Most failed capstones fail in the first week, before a sentence is written, because the candidate has chosen a topic rather than a problem. Wayne Booth and his collaborators draw the distinction precisely in The Craft of Research: a topic is what your essay is about; a problem is the reason your reader should care that you wrote it.[^1] "The afterlife in Spinoza" is a topic. "Why Spinoza's denial of personal immortality has been read, for three centuries, as more consoling than the doctrine it replaced" is a problem. The first invites a survey. The second invites a thesis.

We ask for a problem because the oral defense rewards nothing else. A survey can be interrupted at any point and lose nothing; a thesis, interrupted, must be defended at the point of interruption or conceded. The shape of your essay should be the shape of an argument you are willing to lose — which is the only kind worth winning.

## II. The Architecture of a Defensible Claim

Booth, Colomb, and Williams describe the working parts of an argument as claim, reason, evidence, acknowledgment, and warrant.[^2] The first three are taught in any undergraduate seminar. The last two distinguish a defensible essay from a confident one. Acknowledgment is the explicit hospitality you extend to the strongest version of the position you mean to reject. Warrant is the often-tacit principle that connects your evidence to your claim — the assumption that, if challenged, must be either defended or revised.

> A topic becomes a problem only when you can show your reader why not knowing the answer costs them something.
>
> — Booth, Colomb & Williams, The Craft of Research

In the defense, your examiners will not, as a rule, attack your claim. They will attack your warrants. They will ask why this evidence bears on this conclusion; why the case you have chosen is representative rather than convenient; why the philosopher you have dismissed in a footnote does not, on his own terms, dismiss you in return. Prepare, then, not by memorizing your argument but by reconstructing the argument against it — in its strongest form, charitably stated, and answered without contempt. A capstone that cannot survive its own steel-man is not yet finished.

## III. On Choosing What You Cannot Say Lightly

Choose a question to which you do not already know the answer. This is the single piece of advice on which the full faculty agrees, and the one most often ignored. A capstone written to vindicate a conclusion the candidate held at matriculation tends to read, by the final chapter, like a brief filed on behalf of a younger self. Bertrand Russell, closing The Problems of Philosophy, suggested that the value of the discipline lay not in definite answers but in the questions themselves, which enlarge the mind by being lived with.[^3] Live with yours for the term. Let it change you, or let it survive your attempts to change it.

Over the next weeks you will receive, in sequence, methodological notes from each of the prior faculties — Mackie on the structure of a philosophical case, Rowe on evidential humility, Hume on prose that earns its assent.[^4] Read them as instruments, not as templates. The essay that emerges should be recognizably yours: defensible at every joint, charitable at every turn, and written in a voice you would not be ashamed to read aloud to someone who disagrees.

∴ The defense begins the moment you choose what you cannot say lightly.
