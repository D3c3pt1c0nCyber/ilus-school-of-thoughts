# Religion as Anthropology

*What belief does in the body of a community, before we ask whether it is true.*

In the summer of 1912, a French sociologist published a book about Australian aboriginal totems and, in doing so, changed what it meant to study religion at all. Emile Durkheim had never met an Arrernte clansman, never watched a corroboree under desert stars; he worked from ethnographies sent home by Spencer and Gillen. Yet from that secondhand material he drew a thesis that still unsettles both believers and their critics: that when a community worships its god, it is, without knowing it, worshipping itself. The claim is not a sneer. It is an invitation to look at what religion does before we argue about whether it is true.

## I. Durkheim and the Social Fact

The Elementary Forms of the Religious Life is a strange and patient book. Durkheim wanted to find religion in its simplest documented form, on the theory that what is essential to a thing will show most plainly in its least elaborated version. He chose totemism among the Arrernte and Warlpiri peoples of central Australia, and what he found there, or thought he found, was a structure beneath every cathedral and synagogue and mosque he had ever known. The totem was not, he argued, a god mistakenly assigned to an animal. It was the clan itself, made visible to itself, given a name and a face it could revere.

What Durkheim called the sacred was not, in his account, a property of objects. It was a quality conferred by collective attention. A churinga stone is wood; a host is bread; a flag is cloth. The work of religion is to transfigure the ordinary into the binding, and to do so through ritual gathered bodies, the rhythmic chant, the shared fast, the wedding and the wake. To study religion as a social fact was not to explain it away. It was to notice, at last, what it had been doing all along.

## II. James, Geertz, and the Interior Weather

Durkheim's instrument was the crowd; William James, lecturing at Edinburgh a decade earlier, took up the lone soul. The Varieties of Religious Experience moves through conversions, mystical states, sick souls and healthy-minded ones, the testimonies of Tolstoy and Bunyan and a hundred anonymous correspondents. James was a psychologist by training and a pragmatist by temperament, and he refused the question of whether the mystic had touched anything real. He asked, instead, what the experience did in a life. Did it heal, integrate, sustain? The fruits, not the roots, were what could be examined.

> Religion is a system of symbols that clothes our conceptions of a general order of existence in such an aura of factuality that the moods it produces seem uniquely realistic.
>
> — paraphrased from Clifford Geertz, Religion as a Cultural System (1966)

Clifford Geertz, writing in 1966, gave the discipline its most quoted formulation. Religion, on his account, was a system of symbols that established powerful moods and motivations by formulating conceptions of a general order of existence and clothing those conceptions in such an aura of factuality that the moods seemed uniquely realistic. The sentence is dense, deliberately so. It joins what Durkheim and James had separated: the public symbol and the private weather, the cathedral and the kneeling figure inside it. Religion, in Geertz's reading, is neither only sociology nor only psychology. It is the choreography by which a culture teaches its members how the world feels.

## III. The Cognitive Turn

A generation after Geertz, a new question arrived from an unexpected quarter. If religion is so widespread, so structurally similar across cultures that have never met, perhaps the explanation is not only social but cognitive. Pascal Boyer, in Religion Explained, argued that supernatural concepts succeed because they are minimally counterintuitive: a tree that listens, a person who walks through walls, an ancestor who watches. Such ideas violate one expectation while preserving the rest, and so they are memorable, transmissible, sticky in the mind. The gods, on this account, are not invented; they are selected, by the architecture of human attention.

None of this settles the older questions. To know what a fire does in a hearth is not to know whether it warms a real house. But it does change the shape of the conversation. In the lessons ahead we will turn from what religion does to what it claims, and ask whether the claims survive when their functions are named. For now it is enough to notice that the village that buries its dead together is doing something, whatever else it is doing, that cannot be undone by argument alone.

∴ Before the argument, the gathering.

## Notes

1. Emile Durkheim, The Elementary Forms of the Religious Life, trans. Karen E. Fields (Free Press, 1995; orig. 1912). The Australian material is drawn from Spencer and Gillen's ethnographies of the 1890s.
2. William James, The Varieties of Religious Experience: A Study in Human Nature (Longmans, Green, 1902), delivered as the Gifford Lectures at Edinburgh, 1901-1902. See especially Lectures IX-X on conversion and XVI-XVII on mysticism.
3. Clifford Geertz, 'Religion as a Cultural System,' in Anthropological Approaches to the Study of Religion, ed. Michael Banton (1966); reprinted in The Interpretation of Cultures (Basic Books, 1973).
4. Pascal Boyer, Religion Explained: The Evolutionary Origins of Religious Thought (Basic Books, 2001). On minimal counterintuitiveness, see chapters 2-3.
