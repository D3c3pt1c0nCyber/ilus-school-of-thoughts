# The Quiet Revolution: Secularism in the Modern World

*How modern states learned, unevenly and incompletely, to live without God at the center of their public life.*

In the winter of 1905, the French parliament passed a law of nine articles that did something strange: it pretended nothing had changed. The Republic recognized no religion, salaried no religion, subsidized no religion. Cathedrals would remain standing; bells would still ring across Burgundy at noon. But the state, after eleven centuries of entanglement with the Church, had quietly stepped away. Charles Taylor calls this the "subtraction story," and he means it as a warning. Secularism, when it arrives, rarely arrives loudly. It arrives as a draftsman's revision, a clause inserted, a name removed from a register, a school built on the edge of a parish.

## I. The Wall and Its Architects

The American version came first, and came in a letter. In January 1802, Thomas Jefferson wrote to the Danbury Baptists of Connecticut, who had complained of being a religious minority in a Congregationalist state. His reply borrowed a metaphor from Roger Williams a century earlier: a wall of separation between Church and State. The phrase appears nowhere in the Constitution. It is a gloss, a private interpretation that became, through long citation, a kind of unofficial amendment to the First Amendment itself. The wall was always more porous than the metaphor suggested. Chaplains opened Congress. Currency invoked the deity. Presidents swore on Bibles.

What Jefferson built, and what Madison defended in his Memorial and Remonstrance of 1785, was not the absence of religion from public life but its disestablishment — the refusal of the state to anoint one creed as the creed. This is a thinner claim than the French one, and a thicker one than it appears. It assumes, as Akeel Bilgrami has noticed, that religion can be bracketed without being diminished, that citizens can hold their deepest commitments in one hand while shaking with the other across confessional lines.

## II. Laïcité and Its Discontents

The French inheritance is colder. Where Jefferson imagined a wall, the Third Republic imagined a clearing — a public square swept clean of religious symbol so that citizens might meet there as citizens and nothing else. The 1905 law of separation, drafted by Aristide Briand and Émile Combes, was the legislative end of a century-long quarrel that had begun with the guillotine and ended with the schoolroom. Jules Ferry's education laws of the 1880s had already removed crucifixes from classroom walls and priests from teaching posts. Laïcité was, in this sense, less a doctrine than a habit of governance, learned slowly and at cost.

> Taylor argues that what changed in the modern West was not the answer to the question of God, but the conditions under which the question could be asked at all.
>
> — after Charles Taylor, A Secular Age

Talal Asad has argued, with characteristic patience, that this clearing is not as neutral as it presents itself. To require that religious identity be left at the door of the public square is to require something of religious people that is not required of the secular — namely, that they translate their convictions into a language the state will recognize. The recent French debates over the veil, over conspicuous religious symbols in schools, over the very meaning of the word communautarisme, are not departures from laïcité. They are its working out. The clearing has edges, and the edges have been contested since Briand laid them down.

## III. The Indian Experiment and the Unfinished Business

The Indian model, drafted into the Constitution of 1950 under Ambedkar's chairmanship, is stranger and arguably braver than either. Where the Americans separated and the French cleared, the Indians attempted something Bilgrami has called principled distance — a state that engages with religion in order to reform it, intervening in Hindu temple entry, in Muslim personal law, in Sikh succession, not despite secularism but as its expression. The state stands close enough to touch, and touches in the name of equality. Whether the experiment has survived its first three-quarters of a century is a question we will not settle here.

What we will say, with Taylor and against the triumphalists of either side, is that secularism is not the absence of a worldview. It is a worldview — one with its own metaphysical commitments, its own anthropology, its own quiet sacraments. The next lesson takes up Marx and Feuerbach, who saw this clearly in the nineteenth century and asked a harder question: if religion is a human projection, what is the human projecting from?

∴ The wall, the clearing, the principled distance — three answers to one question, none of them finished.

## Notes

1. Charles Taylor, A Secular Age (Cambridge: Harvard University Press, 2007), especially the introduction and chapters 1–2, on the three senses of "secular" and the critique of subtraction narratives.
2. Talal Asad, Formations of the Secular: Christianity, Islam, Modernity (Stanford: Stanford University Press, 2003), on the anthropology of the secular as a category and its formation alongside, not in opposition to, the religious.
3. Akeel Bilgrami, Secularism, Identity, and Enchantment (Cambridge: Harvard University Press, 2014), on principled distance and the Indian constitutional settlement; see also his earlier essays in Economic and Political Weekly.
4. Jefferson's letter to the Danbury Baptist Association, January 1, 1802, is reprinted in The Papers of Thomas Jefferson, vol. 36; Madison's Memorial and Remonstrance Against Religious Assessments (1785) is the more philosophically rigorous of the founding documents.
