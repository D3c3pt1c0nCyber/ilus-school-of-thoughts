# Why This School Exists

*A letter from the founder on what a school of thoughts is for, and what it refuses to become.*

There is a particular kind of student this school was built for. She was raised inside a faith she has not yet decided how to keep or leave. Or he was raised inside no faith at all, and finds the certainty of his unbelief beginning, late at night, to embarrass him. Or they were taught that the question was settled long ago, in one direction or the other, and have come to suspect that nothing important is ever settled that easily. To all of them, and to the merely curious who arrived by accident: welcome. The doors here are heavy, but they open both ways.

## I. What this school is for

Ilu's School of Thoughts exists to read carefully, in good company, the arguments that have been offered for and against the existence of God, the authority of revelation, and the foundations of moral life without one. It is a school of philosophy in the older sense — a place where one sits with a problem long enough to be changed by it. The curriculum draws from the analytic tradition (Mackie, Rowe, Plantinga, Swinburne), from the continental tradition where it bears on the question (Nietzsche, Weil, Ricoeur), and from the older quarrels that gave the modern ones their shape (Hume's Dialogues, Aquinas's Five Ways, the Book of Job).

What unites the syllabus is not a conclusion but a manner. We read theists as if they meant what they said, because they did. We read atheists the same way. A student who cannot state the strongest version of the position she rejects has not yet earned the right to reject it.

## II. What this school is not for

It is not a debate club, and it is not a deconversion mill. The internet has both in abundance, and they tend, in their different ways, to produce the same student: louder than before, and no wiser. A school that taught contempt for the believing grandmother, or for the village priest who sat with the dying, would be a school not worth attending. Bertrand Russell, whose 1927 lecture gives one of our recurring texts its title, was an unbeliever of the first rank, and also a man who took the trouble to read his opponents in their own languages. That combination — unflinching argument, undefended generosity — is the temperament we are trying to cultivate.

> He who knows only his own side of the case knows little of that. — paraphrasing Mill, On Liberty, ch. II
>
> — John Stuart Mill, On Liberty (1859), Chapter II

Nor is this a school of conclusions. No student here will be asked to believe anything, including the negations of things. You may finish the program a Thomist, a Buddhist, a tired agnostic, a cheerful atheist, or, like most thoughtful people through history, something for which there is not yet a clean name. What you will be asked is to hold your position the way Mill asked his readers to hold theirs: knowing the case against it, and able to say why it has not, in the end, persuaded you.

## III. What you will be asked, and what comes next

You will be asked to read slowly. You will be asked to write the opposing brief before you write your own. You will be asked, when a chapter ends, to sit for a moment with the possibility that the author was right and you were not. This is an old discipline, older than the universities; it belongs to the rabbis and the scholastics and the Greek schools that met under colonnades, and it survives, barely, in a few seminar rooms and a few good books. We are trying to keep a small fire of it alive online.

The lessons that follow begin where modern philosophy of religion begins: with the problem of evil, in William Rowe's spare 1979 formulation of a fawn dying in a forest. From there we move to the design arguments and their critics, to the moral arguments and theirs, to the meaning-of-life literature that emerged when the older meanings began to thin. Russell will return. So will Hume, and Job, and Simone Weil, who believed and suffered, and who reminds us why this is not a parlour game.

∴ The door is heavy. Push.

## Notes

1. Bertrand Russell, "Why I Am Not a Christian," lecture delivered to the National Secular Society, South London Branch, 6 March 1927. Russell's example remains a model of polemic that does not slide into caricature.
2. John Stuart Mill, On Liberty (London: John W. Parker and Son, 1859), Chapter II, "Of the Liberty of Thought and Discussion." The argument that we hold even true opinions as dead dogma when we cannot defend them against objection is the closest thing this school has to a founding charter.
3. William L. Rowe, "The Problem of Evil and Some Varieties of Atheism," American Philosophical Quarterly 16, no. 4 (October 1979): 335–341. The first lesson of the main sequence takes Rowe's fawn as its starting image.
4. On the temperament described here — argument without contempt — see also Mill's remark in the same chapter that the worst offence against argument is not the holding of a wrong opinion but the stigmatising of those who hold the contrary one.
