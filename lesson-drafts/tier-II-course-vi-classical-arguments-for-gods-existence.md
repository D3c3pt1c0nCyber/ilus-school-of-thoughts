# The Classical Arguments for God's Existence

*Four arguments, four centuries, and the discipline of reading believers as if their reasoning still mattered to us.*

In the spring of 1078, in a Benedictine cell at Bec, a man who would later die Archbishop of Canterbury set down a sentence he believed had been given to him after long sleeplessness, almost as a relief. Anselm called the small book that contained it Proslogion — an address — and the sentence inside it has occupied philosophers for nine centuries. We begin here, with a monk's insomnia and a definition, because the four arguments before us deserve to be read the way their authors wrote them: slowly, in good faith, and as if something were at stake.

## I. Anselm in his cell

The ontological argument is the strangest of the four because it requires nothing of the world. Anselm proposes a definition — God as that than which nothing greater can be conceived — and then asks whether such a being could exist only in the understanding. If it did, a greater being could be conceived: the same one, existing also in reality. The definition would then refer to something other than itself. Existence, on this reasoning, is implicit in the concept correctly held.[^1]

Gaunilo, a contemporary monk, replied with the example of a perfect island and was answered, fairly or not, that islands are not the kind of thing whose perfection entails existence. Kant later objected that existence is not a predicate. Both replies have force. What is harder to dismiss is the seriousness of Anselm's procedure: he is not arguing from the world to God but examining what we have already said when we use the word at all.

## II. The Five Ways and the watch

Aquinas wrote in Paris in the 1260s, and his Five Ways occupy less than a page of the Summa Theologica.[^2] They argue from motion, from causation, from contingency, from gradation, and from the governance of things — each beginning in experience and ending in what he calls, with characteristic restraint, that which all men speak of as God. The arguments are cosmological in spirit: they ask why there is anything rather than nothing, and why the something behaves as it does. Whether the chain of causes must terminate, and whether its terminus must be personal, are the questions every later philosopher has had to answer.

> That than which nothing greater can be conceived.
>
> — Anselm, Proslogion (c. 1078)

William Paley, writing in 1802, made the teleological case in the only image anyone remembers.[^3] A man crossing a heath finds a watch. He does not assume, as he might of a stone, that it has lain there forever; the arrangement of its parts for an end implies a watchmaker. Darwin, half a century later, would offer a mechanism by which arrangement for an end could arise without one — but Paley's question, properly stated, is not whether design has a natural explanation but whether the kind of order we observe requires an explanation at all.

## III. Plantinga, and the argument from conscience

Alvin Plantinga, in 1974, gave the ontological argument a modal reformulation: if a maximally great being is possible — exists in some possible world — then by the logic of necessity it exists in every possible world, including ours.[^4] The argument is valid. Its premise, that maximal greatness is possible, is precisely what an atheist will deny, and Plantinga concedes that the argument does not compel assent so much as display the cost of denying it. This is, I think, the honest condition of all four arguments. They do not coerce; they articulate.

The moral argument — that objective moral obligation requires a lawgiver — we will take up next week in its own right, since it depends less on metaphysics than on what we are willing to say about the binding force of conscience. For now, hold the four together. Read Anselm, Aquinas, Paley, and Plantinga as colleagues across centuries, working a difficult problem with the tools they had. The refutations will come. They are better refutations when the arguments have been understood.

∴ To meet an argument with seriousness is the highest form of disagreement.

## Notes

1. Anselm, Proslogion, ch. II–IV (c. 1078). The argument is conducted as a prayer; this is not incidental to its method.
2. Thomas Aquinas, Summa Theologica I.2.3 (c. 1265–1274). The Five Ways are framed as responses to the question whether God's existence can be demonstrated.
3. William Paley, Natural Theology, ch. I (1802). The watch is found upon a heath; the question is what the heath does not explain.
4. Alvin Plantinga, The Nature of Necessity, ch. X (Oxford, 1974). The modal reformulation requires that a maximally great being exists in some possible world.
