# The Enlightenment and Its Inheritors

*On Spinoza's grindstone, Kant's wager, and what the eighteenth century asked of us that we have not yet finished answering.*

In a room above a quiet street in The Hague, Baruch Spinoza ground lenses for a living and, in the hours between commissions, ground theology into something unrecognizable. He died in 1677, two decades before the century we now call the Enlightenment began in earnest, and yet almost every quarrel of that century is already audible in his unfinished sentences. To read Spinoza is to feel a strange compression: the future arrives before its furniture. The men who came after him — Voltaire, Diderot, d'Holbach, Kant — inherited not a finished doctrine but a permission, and we have not yet finished spending it.

## I. The radical and the moderate

Jonathan Israel's great service, across the volumes that begin with Radical Enlightenment, was to insist that there were two Enlightenments, not one. The moderate stream — Locke, Newton in his theological mood, the Anglican latitudinarians — sought to reconcile reason with revelation and monarchy with constitution. The radical stream, descending from Spinoza's Ethics through the clandestine French manuscripts of the early eighteenth century, sought no such reconciliation. It proposed instead a single substance, a natural morality, a democratic politics, and a press free enough to argue about all three. The distinction matters because it changes the genealogy of the modern.

When Voltaire writes against the infamous, when Diderot edits the Encyclopédie in the teeth of censors, when the Baron d'Holbach hosts dinners that scandalize visiting clergymen, we are watching the radical stream surface. It does not yet win. But its vocabulary becomes, by 1789, the only vocabulary in which European politics can still be conducted without embarrassment.

## II. Kant's small wager

In December of 1784, in the Berlinische Monatsschrift, Kant offered a definition that has been quoted to the point of transparency and is therefore worth looking at again. Enlightenment, he wrote, is the human being's emergence from a self-incurred minority — a Unmündigkeit, a condition of needing someone else to think on one's behalf. The fault, he insisted with characteristic severity, lies not in the lack of understanding but in the lack of resolve. Sapere aude. Dare to know.

> Enlightenment is the human being's emergence from a self-incurred minority — and the fault, Kant insists, lies not in want of understanding but in want of resolve.
>
> — after Kant, 'Beantwortung der Frage: Was ist Aufklärung?', 1784

The wager embedded in this small essay is enormous. Kant is betting that public reason, given room, will discipline itself; that argument among adults, conducted without coercion, tends toward the true; that the priest and the censor and the bureaucrat are not, in the long run, necessary. He hedges the wager carefully — obey the king, he says, but argue freely in print — and the hedge has been mocked. The mockery, I think, misses what is brave about the formulation. Kant is asking whether a species long accustomed to tutelage can, in fact, walk.

## III. The long shadow and its critics

The wager has not been settled. In 1944, in a Los Angeles exile from the catastrophe that European reason had failed to prevent, Theodor Adorno and Max Horkheimer drafted the bitter pages that became Dialectic of Enlightenment. Their charge was not that the philosophes had been wrong to demand the disenchantment of the world, but that disenchantment, left to its own logic, becomes a new mythology — instrumental, administrative, indifferent to the ends it serves. Auschwitz, they argued, was not the negation of the Enlightenment but one of its possibilities. Alasdair MacIntyre, writing After Virtue in 1981, brought a different indictment: that the project of grounding morality in reason alone, having severed itself from the Aristotelian and Christian traditions that gave moral terms their content, could only end in emotivism and managerial drift.

Postcolonial critics have added a third charge, harder to dismiss than either: that the universal reason proclaimed in Paris and Königsberg was, in practice, a reason that wrote slave codes and partitioned continents. None of this settles the question. It sharpens it. In the lessons that follow, we will sit with each of these critiques in turn — and ask what, if anything, of the eighteenth century's permission still belongs to us.

∴ The eighteenth century opened a door. We are still in the hallway.

## Notes

1. Jonathan Israel, Radical Enlightenment: Philosophy and the Making of Modernity 1650–1750 (Oxford: Oxford University Press, 2001). The two-streams thesis is developed across the trilogy that continues with Enlightenment Contested (2006) and Democratic Enlightenment (2011).
2. Immanuel Kant, 'Beantwortung der Frage: Was ist Aufklärung?', Berlinische Monatsschrift, December 1784. The Latin tag sapere aude — dare to know — Kant borrows from Horace, Epistles I.2.
3. Theodor W. Adorno and Max Horkheimer, Dialektik der Aufklärung (drafted 1944, first published Amsterdam, 1947). The thesis that 'myth is already enlightenment, and enlightenment reverts to mythology' is stated in the first chapter.
4. Alasdair MacIntyre, After Virtue: A Study in Moral Theory (Notre Dame: University of Notre Dame Press, 1981). MacIntyre's diagnosis of emotivism as the unintended heir of the Enlightenment moral project is laid out in chapters 1–3.
