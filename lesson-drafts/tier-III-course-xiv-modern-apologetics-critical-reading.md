# Modern Apologetics — A Critical Reading

*On reading Craig, Plantinga, Hart, and Feser as one would read any serious philosopher — at length, and on their own terms.*

There is a peculiar discipline in reading carefully the writers one is supposed to have dispensed with. Open Edward Feser's Five Proofs of the Existence of God and you will not find the strawman of campus debate; you will find a Thomist of considerable patience, walking the reader through act and potency as a mathematician walks a student through a proof. The honest atheist owes these books a real hearing. Not the courtesy of caricature — the harder courtesy of close reading. What follows is a syllabus for that reading, and a warning about what one finds when the texts are taken at full strength.

## I. Four Books, Four Postures

William Lane Craig's Reasonable Faith is, before it is anything else, a piece of pedagogy. It teaches the kalām cosmological argument as a craftsman teaches a joint: premises numbered, objections rehearsed, the Borde-Guth-Vilenkin theorem deployed like a finishing nail. Alvin Plantinga's Warranted Christian Belief is the opposite temperament — slow, technical, more interested in epistemology than apologetics, asking under what conditions a belief in God could be properly basic, held without the scaffolding of argument at all. Read consecutively, the two books quarrel quietly. Craig wants you persuaded; Plantinga wants you to see that you may already, rationally, be there.

David Bentley Hart's The Experience of God refuses the genre entirely. Hart is not arguing for the God of the philosophers' debating societies, that demiurgic engineer the New Atheists love to dismantle. He is arguing that the classical traditions — Christian, Vedantic, Sufi — name something the modern apologist and the modern atheist have together forgotten how to see. Edward Feser, in Five Proofs, completes the quartet by reviving the scholastic arguments at their full medieval strength.[^1]

## II. What Survives the Charitable Reading

The honest report is that several of these arguments survive their hostile summaries handily. The kalām, taken seriously, is not the schoolyard syllogism atheists tend to remember. Craig's defense rests on a genuinely difficult question in the philosophy of mathematics — whether an actual infinite can be instantiated in concrete reality — and the question remains open in the literature.[^2] Plantinga's reformed epistemology is more unsettling still: it does not try to prove God so much as dismantle the evidentialist demand that proof be the price of rational belief. One may reject his conclusions and still concede that the framework around the rejection has shifted.

> God, in Hart's reading, is not a thing alongside other things but the sheer act of being by which anything is at all.
>
> — after David Bentley Hart, The Experience of God

Hart is the most difficult to answer because he refuses to play the game the atheist polemicist has set up. When he writes that God is not a being among beings but Being itself, the sheer ground of the existence of anything at all, he is not being evasive; he is restating what Aquinas and Maimonides and Ibn Sina each said in their own idioms.[^3] To answer Hart, one must engage classical metaphysics — act and potency, essence and existence, the contingency of finite things — rather than the cartoon deism that has, for two centuries, been the easier target.

## III. Where the Reading Leaves Us

A charitable reading is not a conversion narrative. The arguments survive their summaries; they do not, in the judgment of this reader, survive their best objections. Feser's revival of the Aristotelian proofs depends on a metaphysics of causal series that contemporary physics has rendered, at minimum, contestable. Plantinga's properly basic belief in God turns out to be properly basic for the believer in a way it is not for the Hindu, the Yoruba diviner, or the Mormon — a parity problem he acknowledges but does not, I think, resolve. Hart's classical theism is the most formidable of the four, and the one against which the strongest atheist response is not argument but the long patience of phenomenology: the world, attended to closely, does not announce itself as held in being by anything.

We will spend the next four lessons reading these books seriously. In Lesson xv, the kalām at full strength, with the Hilbert's Hotel objections and the BGV theorem. In xvi, Plantinga and the parity problem. In xvii, Hart and the question of Being. In xviii, Feser and the scholastic revival. Bring the books. Bring patience. The case against theism is only worth making against the strongest version of the thing.

∴ The strongest argument against a position is the one its best defender would recognize.

## Notes

1. Edward Feser, Five Proofs of the Existence of God (Ignatius Press, 2017). Feser argues that the Aristotelian, Neoplatonic, Augustinian, Thomistic, and Rationalist proofs each succeed on their own terms and converge on a single classical conception of God.
2. William Lane Craig, Reasonable Faith: Christian Truth and Apologetics, 3rd ed. (Crossway, 2008). The kalām is developed at length in chapter 3; the Hilbert's Hotel argument against the actual infinite remains the load-bearing element.
3. David Bentley Hart, The Experience of God: Being, Consciousness, Bliss (Yale University Press, 2013). Hart's central polemic is that contemporary atheism and contemporary apologetics share a common error: treating God as a discrete entity within the cosmos rather than as the ground of its being.
4. Alvin Plantinga, Warranted Christian Belief (Oxford University Press, 2000). The third and concluding volume of the warrant trilogy; the Aquinas/Calvin model of properly basic theistic belief is developed in Part III.
