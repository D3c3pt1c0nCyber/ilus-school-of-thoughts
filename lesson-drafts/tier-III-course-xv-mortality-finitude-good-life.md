# A Long Conversation With Death

*On Lucretius, Epictetus, and the modern psychologists who measured what the ancients only counseled.*

In a Roman winter near the end of the Republic, a poet named Titus Lucretius Carus set himself the task of arguing his readers out of their fear of death. He wrote in hexameter, because the medicine was bitter and the cup needed honey on its rim. Two thousand years later, in a laboratory in Tucson, three social psychologists were asking subjects to contemplate their own funerals and then measuring, in milliseconds, how much more harshly those subjects judged a stranger who disagreed with them. The distance between the poem and the experiment is the distance this lesson means to cross.

## I. The Ancient Consolation

Lucretius, following Epicurus, offered an argument so clean it has the look of a proof. Death is the dissolution of the compound that was you; when death is present, you are not; when you are present, death is not; therefore death is nothing to us, and the fear of it is a fear of nothing. Book III of De Rerum Natura presses this case for nine hundred lines, accumulating images — the banquet one has had enough of, the traveller who lays down his pack — until the reader is meant to feel, not merely concede, that to dread non-being is to dread the long quiet before one was born, which no one dreads at all.

Epictetus, writing in Greek a century later and from the very different vantage of a freed slave, reached a kindred conclusion by a different road. What disturbs us, he insisted in the Discourses, is not events but our judgments of events; and death, judged rightly, is among the things indifferent. The Stoic and the Epicurean disagreed about almost everything else — atoms, gods, pleasure, providence — yet on this they converged: the well-lived life requires a settled relation to its ending, and that relation is the work of philosophy, daily and unromantic, like tending a fire.

## II. The Worm at the Core

The ancients counselled. The moderns measured. In 1973, an American cultural anthropologist named Ernest Becker, dying of cancer in a Vancouver hospital, published The Denial of Death, a book stitched together from Kierkegaard, Otto Rank, and Freud, and animated by a single audacious claim: that the awareness of mortality is the engine beneath nearly all human striving, and that civilization itself is an elaborate apparatus for keeping that awareness at bay. Heroism, religion, art, nationalism, even the small daily insistences of the ego — Becker read them all as attempts to deny, by symbolic means, what the body cannot help knowing.

> Civilization itself, Becker proposed, is a vast and ingenious apparatus for the denial of death.
>
> — paraphrased from Ernest Becker, The Denial of Death (1973)

Becker died the year his book won the Pulitzer. A decade later, three young psychologists — Sheldon Solomon, Jeff Greenberg, and Tom Pyszczynski — set out to test what they took to be his hypothesis, and in doing so invented what is now called Terror Management Theory. Their paradigm was simple and, in its results, unnerving. Remind a person of their mortality, however briefly, and you will reliably observe a shift: stronger identification with one's in-group, harsher judgments of those who threaten one's worldview, increased deference to charismatic authority. The findings have replicated, with the usual qualifications, across hundreds of studies and several decades.[^1]

## III. Living With the Knowledge

What follows, then, if Becker and his heirs are roughly right? Not, I think, the easy secular triumph some readers reach for. If the fear of death drives our worldview defenses, the atheist is not exempt; we too cling, we too harden when threatened, we too build small immortalities out of careers and causes and the approval of strangers. The Worm at the Core is not a brief against religion. It is a brief against unexamined consolation of every kind, and it leaves the older question — how shall one live, knowing what one knows? — exactly where Lucretius and Epictetus left it.[^2]

The course turns next to what the ancients called the ars moriendi, the art of dying, and to its contemporary descendants in palliative medicine, hospice philosophy, and the small but serious literature on what psychologists now call mortality salience interventions. We will read Montaigne on Cicero, Atul Gawande on his father, and a 2014 study in which contemplating death, under the right framing, produced not defensive hostility but a measurable increase in gratitude.[^3] The fawn in Rowe's forest dies unwitnessed. We do not. That, for the philosopher, is the beginning of the work, not its end.

∴ The cup, the honey, the bitter medicine. We drink.

## Notes

1. Sheldon Solomon, Jeff Greenberg, and Tom Pyszczynski, The Worm at the Core: On the Role of Death in Life (Random House, 2015). The foundational experimental papers date to the mid-1980s; the book is their accessible synthesis.
2. Lucretius, De Rerum Natura, Book III, lines 830 onward, for the argument that death is nihil ad nos — nothing to us. Epictetus, Discourses, especially I.1 and II.1, on the distinction between things in our power and things indifferent.
3. Ernest Becker, The Denial of Death (Free Press, 1973), which won the Pulitzer Prize for General Nonfiction in 1974, two months after Becker's death. Whether his synthesis of Rank and Kierkegaard is finally correct is a question we will revisit; that it has proven generative is no longer in dispute.
