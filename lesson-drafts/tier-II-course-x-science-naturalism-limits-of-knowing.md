# Science, Naturalism, and the Limits of Knowing

*On the difference between a method that has served us well and a worldview that flatters itself by borrowing the method's prestige.*

In 1740, a young Scotsman in a Jesuit town in France finished a book he believed would topple the metaphysicians, and then watched it, in his own phrase, fall dead-born from the press. David Hume was twenty-eight, and he had not yet learned that the most dangerous arguments often arrive quietly. The Treatise had asked an unsettling question: when we say the sun will rise tomorrow, on what authority do we speak? Not on the authority of logic, Hume answered, and not on the authority of the senses. On the authority, rather, of habit. The empirical edifice begins, it turns out, on a foundation made of expectation.

## I. Two Naturalisms, Often Confused

There is a distinction philosophers learn to make early, and that the public rarely encounters at all. Methodological naturalism is a working rule of the laboratory: when you investigate the natural world, restrict your explanations to natural causes. It is a discipline, not a doctrine. The geologist who declines to invoke angels in accounting for the strata of the Grand Canyon is not thereby denying the existence of angels; she is simply doing geology. Metaphysical naturalism is the larger claim that the natural world is all there is, that no being or principle stands outside it. The first is a methodological hygiene. The second is a philosophical position, defensible on its own terms but not, crucially, established by the practice of science itself.

Conflating the two is the most common error in popular discussion of science and religion, and it travels in both directions. The believer who accuses science of having ruled God out by fiat is mistaking method for metaphysics. The atheist who claims science has proven naturalism is making the same mistake from the opposite shore. Neither side has read Hume carefully, which is to say, neither side has yet noticed that the question of what science can establish is itself not a scientific question.

## II. Popper's Asymmetry, Kuhn's Revolution

Karl Popper, writing in Vienna in 1934, proposed a deceptively modest reform. Science, he said, does not proceed by accumulating confirmations. A thousand white swans do not prove the proposition that all swans are white; one black swan, found in Australia in 1697, refutes it. The asymmetry between verification and falsification is, for Popper, the engine of inquiry. A theory worth taking seriously is one that forbids something, that tells us what we should not observe if it is true. Theories that explain everything explain nothing. This was Popper's polite, devastating verdict on the great unfalsifiable systems of his century.

> Science is the best tool we have for understanding the natural world, but it cannot answer every meaningful question we are capable of asking.
>
> — paraphrasing Massimo Pigliucci, Nonsense on Stilts (2010)

Thomas Kuhn, three decades later, complicated the picture. Scientists do not, as Popper had it, abandon their theories the moment a recalcitrant fact appears. They patch, defer, ignore, propose auxiliary hypotheses. They do this for long stretches Kuhn called normal science, until anomalies accumulate to the point of crisis, and a new framework, a new paradigm, displaces the old. The replacement is rarely a clean logical deduction. It is, Kuhn argued, something closer to a conversion. This is not a charge of irrationality. It is a description of how rational beings, working at the edge of what is known, actually behave.

## III. The Temptation Called Scientism

Massimo Pigliucci has spent much of his career on the boundary where good science shades into something else. The something else he calls scientism: the conviction that science is not merely a powerful method of inquiry but the only legitimate form of knowledge, and that questions it cannot address are therefore not real questions. The position has the seductive clarity of an axe. It also has the axe's tendency to flatten what it strikes. Ethics, aesthetics, the interpretation of a poem, the meaning of a friendship, the question of why there is something rather than nothing: these are not laboratory problems, and treating them as such does not dignify them. It dissolves them.

The honest naturalist holds two thoughts at once. The first is that the methods of the sciences, refined over four centuries, have given us a more reliable picture of the physical world than any rival enterprise in human history. The second is that this fact does not, on its own, settle the metaphysical question. In the lessons ahead we will turn to those metaphysical questions directly, beginning with the cosmological argument and the deep peculiarity of asking why anything exists at all.

∴ The method is a lantern, not a sun.

## Notes

1. David Hume, An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding (1748), especially Section IV, on the problem of induction. The earlier Treatise of Human Nature (1739-40) develops the argument at greater length.
2. Karl Popper, The Logic of Scientific Discovery (Logik der Forschung, 1934; English edition 1959). The criterion of falsifiability is introduced in the opening chapters.
3. Thomas Kuhn, The Structure of Scientific Revolutions (University of Chicago Press, 1962). The terms normal science, paradigm, and anomaly are introduced in chapters II through VI.
4. Massimo Pigliucci, Nonsense on Stilts: How to Tell Science from Bunk (University of Chicago Press, 2010). The discussion of scientism appears most directly in the later chapters.
