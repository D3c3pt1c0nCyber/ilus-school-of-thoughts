# Ethics Without Eternity

*On whether the good survives the death of its guarantor, and what we owe one another in the meantime.*

In the spring of 1880, Ivan Karamazov sat across from his younger brother in a tavern and proposed a worry that has haunted moral philosophy ever since: if God does not exist, everything is permitted. The line is often quoted as if Dostoevsky meant it as argument rather than as fever. He did not. He meant it as a temptation — the sort one entertains at two in the morning, in a city one does not love, with a brother one cannot quite reach. The question that survives the novel, and outlives the century that followed it, is whether the temptation was correct. Can a person be good, in any sense worth the word, without eternity to underwrite the goodness?

## I. The Inheritance We Did Not Ask For

Western moral philosophy did not begin in a church. It began in a gymnasium in Athens, with a man who had buried two wives and was raising a son he would not live to see grown. Aristotle's Nicomachean Ethics — composed, we think, in the late 330s BCE — opens with a claim so quiet it is easy to miss: every action aims at some good.[^1] No revelation is invoked. No divine ledger is consulted. The argument proceeds, for ten books, by attending to what human beings actually do when they are flourishing, and what they fail to do when they are not.

This is the inheritance the secular ethicist receives, and it is older than the religion that later absorbed it. Aquinas baptized Aristotle in the thirteenth century; he did not invent him. When Alasdair MacIntyre, in 1981, argued that we are the heirs of a moral vocabulary whose theological scaffolding has been quietly removed, he was not announcing a catastrophe. He was naming a condition. The question is what one builds in the cleared space.

## II. Three Roads from Königsberg, Athens, and London

Three traditions present themselves, and each refuses the help of heaven. The first is Aristotelian: the good life is the life of a creature functioning well, exercising the capacities proper to its kind. Virtue is a habit, cultivated as a violinist cultivates a bow-hand, and eudaimonia — that untranslatable word, sometimes rendered as flourishing — is its reward and its evidence. The second is Kantian: morality is what a rational being legislates for itself, and the moral law is binding because reason is binding, not because anyone is watching. The third is utilitarian, articulated most patiently by John Stuart Mill in 1861: actions are right in proportion as they tend to promote happiness, and the happiness in question is everyone's, counted without favoritism.[^2]

> The question is not whether one can be good without God, but whether the goodness one had was ever God's to give.
>
> — after Erik Wielenberg, Value and Virtue in a Godless Universe (2005)

Each tradition has its embarrassments. Aristotle owned slaves and thought women incapable of full deliberation. Kant's categorical imperative has been accused of producing answers that any decent person would reject. Mill's calculus seems, at moments, to license horrors if the arithmetic comes out right. These are not small problems. But none of them are problems that theism solves; they are problems that any serious ethics must inherit and work through.

## III. What Remains When the Scaffolding Comes Down

Derek Parfit, in the closing pages of Reasons and Persons (1984), confessed something that sits oddly with his reputation for cold analysis. He wrote that non-religious ethics was a young subject — that we had been thinking carefully about it for only a few generations, and that we should not expect to have finished.[^3] The thought is bracing. The Sistine Chapel was painted in four years; the secular ethical tradition, in its self-conscious form, has had perhaps two centuries, and most of those spent answering objections rather than building.

Erik Wielenberg, writing in 2005, put the constructive case as plainly as anyone has: certain things — the badness of a child's suffering, the goodness of a kept promise — are simply true, and require no further author than the facts themselves.[^4] Whether this is finally defensible is the work of later lessons. For now it is enough to notice that the question we began with — whether one can be good without God — has quietly inverted. The harder question, the one Aristotle was already asking before any of our gods arrived, is what goodness is. We will spend the rest of the term there.

∴ The scaffolding comes down. The building, if there is one, stands.

## Notes

1. Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics, Book I, ch. 1 (1094a). The translation is conventionally Ross's; the Greek is τἀγαθόν.
2. John Stuart Mill, Utilitarianism (London: Parker, Son, and Bourn, 1861), ch. 2. Mill is at pains to distinguish his view from the cruder Benthamite version he had been raised on.
3. Derek Parfit, Reasons and Persons (Oxford: Clarendon Press, 1984), concluding chapter. The remark on the youth of secular ethics appears in the book's final pages and is among the most quoted in the literature.
4. Erik J. Wielenberg, Value and Virtue in a Godless Universe (Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 2005). Wielenberg defends what he calls non-natural, non-theistic moral realism.
