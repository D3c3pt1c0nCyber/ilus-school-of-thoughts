# The Word "Atheism": Definitions, Distinctions, and Dispelling Myths

*On the quiet violence of a word that has been made to carry more than it ought, and the patient work of giving it back its weight.*

There is a word that arrives in the room before the person who uses it, and that word is atheism. It walks in already dressed — sometimes in the rags of nineteenth-century scandal, sometimes in the lab coat of the New Atheists, sometimes in the gentler attire of a Sunday morning spent reading instead of kneeling. Before we can think with the word, we have to undress it. We have to ask what, precisely, a person is saying when they say they are an atheist, and what, precisely, they are not. The history of philosophy is, in part, the history of disputes that turned out to be quarrels over the meanings of nouns.

## I. The Privative and the Positive

The Greek prefix is older than the quarrel. Alpha-privative — the small negation that turns gnosis into agnosis, theos into atheos — does not, by itself, declare a thesis. It withholds one. Antony Flew, writing in 1976, asked us to hear the word atheism with that ancient ear: not as the bold counter-claim "there is no God," but as the simpler refusal, "I am without belief in God." On this reading, an infant is, trivially, an atheist; so is anyone for whom the relevant claim has never been made vivid enough to assent to. This is what we now call negative or weak atheism — a position about one's own credences, not about the furniture of the universe.

Set beside it stands positive or strong atheism, which does make the larger claim: that no such being exists, or that the probability of such a being is vanishingly small. George Smith, writing in 1979, drew the line as implicit versus explicit; Graham Oppy, in our own century, prefers to speak of those who merely lack the belief and those who hold the contrary belief with reasons. The distinction is not pedantic. Whole debates have been lost because one party defended a thesis the other never advanced.

## II. The Company a Word Keeps

Around atheism, like attendants around a difficult guest, stand several other terms. Agnosticism, coined by T. H. Huxley in 1869, is the position that the question of God's existence is unknown, or unknowable in principle; it is a thesis about knowledge, not about belief, and so it can coexist with either atheism or theism in the same mind. One can be an agnostic atheist — unconvinced and unsure — without contradiction. Secularism, by contrast, makes no metaphysical claim at all: it is a political proposal about the relation between religious institutions and civic life, and a devout Catholic can, and many do, defend it.

> If I claimed a china teapot orbited the sun between Earth and Mars, too small for any telescope to see, no one would think it intolerant of them to disbelieve me. The burden, Russell suggested, falls on the one who furnishes the room.
>
> — Bertrand Russell, paraphrased from "Is There a God?" (1952)

Humanism is older and stranger than its modern signage suggests, reaching back through the Renaissance to a confidence in human moral capacity that has been held by believers and unbelievers alike. Antitheism is the position that the existence of God, were it the case, would itself be a misfortune — a stance one can find in Bakunin, in Camus's gentler moments, in Hitchens at his most combative. None of these words is a synonym for the others. To confuse them is to mistake a neighborhood for a single house, and then to be surprised that the inhabitants do not all answer to the same name.

## III. Why the Definitions Matter

A reader may wonder whether all of this is bookkeeping — the kind of taxonomy that interests philosophers and embarrasses everyone else. It is not. Definitions decide who carries the burden of proof, and the burden of proof decides how a conversation will go. If atheism is the bold claim that no God exists, then the atheist owes the room an argument; if atheism is the simple absence of belief, then the theist owes the argument, and the atheist owes only attention. Flew thought the latter — that there is a presumption of atheism in the way there is a presumption of innocence, not because the accused is virtuous, but because the alternative is unworkable.

The lessons that follow in this course will assume the analytic discipline these terms make possible. We will read Rowe on the fawn, Mackie on omnipotence, Hume on miracles, Nietzsche on the death of a particular kind of God. Each of these arguments lives or dies by the precision of its claims. Before we can disagree well, we have to know what, exactly, we are disagreeing about — and what, just as importantly, we are not.

∴ Before we can disagree, we must agree on what we are disagreeing about.

## Notes

1. Antony Flew, The Presumption of Atheism (London: Elek/Pemberton, 1976). Flew's distinction between the privative "a-theist" and the assertive "anti-theist" is the seed of nearly every clarification that follows in the analytic literature.
2. Bertrand Russell, "Is There a God?" (1952), commissioned but unpublished by Illustrated magazine; later collected in The Collected Papers of Bertrand Russell, Vol. 11. The celestial teapot appears here, deployed not as ridicule but as a parable about the location of the burden of proof.
3. George H. Smith, Atheism: The Case Against God (Buffalo, NY: Prometheus Books, 1979). Smith's chapter on the scope of atheism remains the cleanest popular treatment of the implicit/explicit divide, even where one resists his polemical edge.
4. Graham Oppy, Atheism and Agnosticism (Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 2018), Elements in the Philosophy of Religion. Oppy is, in our age, the philosopher most insistent that atheism be argued for rather than merely declared.
