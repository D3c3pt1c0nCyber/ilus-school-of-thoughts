# The Architecture of Belonging

*On what the churches did well, what the atheists have not yet learned to do, and the patient work of assembly without creed.*

On a January morning in 2013, in a deconsecrated church in Islington, two London comedians named Sanderson Jones and Pippa Evans led a roomful of strangers in singing Stevie Wonder. There was no liturgy, no scripture, no god. There was tea afterward. The Sunday Assembly, as they called it, spread within eighteen months to nearly seventy cities. Some commentators sneered; others wept. The interesting question was never whether such a gathering counted as religion. The interesting question was why, a century and a half after Comte, after Adler, after the long secular nineteenth century, we were still inventing the wheel.

## I. What the Sociologist Saw

Emile Durkheim, writing on the elementary forms of religious life in 1912, made an argument that has aged better than most of its contemporaries. Religion, he proposed, was not in the first instance a theory about the cosmos. It was a technology of collective effervescence — a way that a community gathered itself, performed itself, and renewed itself through rhythmic, repeated, embodied practice. The totem was not the point. The dance around the totem was the point. What survived the dance was the society that had danced.

If Durkheim was right, then the secular question becomes uncomfortable. One may abandon the metaphysics of a tradition with relative ease; libraries are full of arguments for doing so. What one cannot abandon, except at considerable cost, is the human appetite for shared time, shared song, shared mourning, shared meals at the long table. Phil Zuckerman's fieldwork in Denmark and Sweden — two of the least religious societies on earth — found populations that were neither anxious nor adrift, but he also found that they had inherited centuries of folk-Lutheran scaffolding, civic and seasonal, which they continued to use without quite noticing.[^1]

## II. Felix Adler and the Long Apprenticeship

The first serious American attempt to build something on this ground was made in 1876, by a young rabbi's son named Felix Adler, who had returned from Heidelberg unable in good conscience to ascend his father's pulpit. The Society for Ethical Culture, which he founded on West Sixty-fourth Street in Manhattan, proposed a deceptively radical thesis: that moral seriousness did not require theological agreement, and that congregations could be organized around the practice of ethical life rather than the affirmation of shared belief. Adler called this 'deed before creed.' He meant it as an austerity, and also as an invitation.

> Deed before creed — the proposition that moral community could be organized around the practice of ethical life rather than agreement about its metaphysical foundations.
>
> — after Felix Adler, Society for Ethical Culture, 1876

What Ethical Culture discovered, over its long and modest century, is that the apprenticeship is harder than the founding. A congregation requires not only conviction but custodians — people willing to make the coffee, run the children's program, sit with the grieving, show up on Sunday when attendance is thin and the rain is steady. The Sunday Assembly movement, more than a hundred years later, would relearn this lesson at speed. Several of its chapters splintered within five years over questions of governance and tone. Bart Campolo, who left evangelical ministry in 2014 to serve as a humanist chaplain at the University of Southern California, has spoken candidly about how much of the pastoral craft he had to keep, and how much he had to build anew without the theological girders that once held it up.[^2]

## III. The Work That Remains

It is tempting, surveying this landscape, to conclude either too much or too little. Too much: that secular community is impossible, that the cathedrals will always out-sing the assembly halls, that one cannot have ritual without the gods who once authorized it. Too little: that the whole business is a sentimental hangover, and that mature unbelievers should be content with friendship, family, and the occasional public lecture. Both conclusions seem to me premature. The Ethical Culture societies are still meeting. The humanist chaplaincies are growing. Sunday Assembly, chastened, continues. None of this constitutes a replacement for what was lost, and perhaps that framing is itself the error.

What it constitutes, instead, is an apprenticeship — the slow human work of learning to gather without the inherited architecture, to mark birth and death and the turning of the year without borrowed scripts, to sit in a room with strangers and become, over time, something other than strangers. The next lesson will turn to ritual proper: to what we lose when we lose the funeral, the blessing, the threshold rite, and what the secular imagination has so far managed to put in their place.

∴ The dance, not the totem.

## Notes

1. Phil Zuckerman, Society Without God (New York University Press, 2008). Zuckerman's interviews in Aarhus and Copenhagen remain the most careful empirical account of what 'living without God' looks like at the population scale, and his observation that Scandinavian secularity rests on inherited Lutheran civic forms is essential to any honest reckoning.
2. Bart Campolo's reflections on the transition from evangelical to humanist ministry have appeared in numerous interviews and in his podcast Humanize Me; the comparison with his father Tony Campolo, who remained in evangelical ministry, gives the conversation a rare intergenerational texture.
3. Emile Durkheim, The Elementary Forms of Religious Life (1912; trans. Karen Fields, Free Press, 1995). The relevant argument is concentrated in Book II, chapters 6 and 7, on the social function of ritual and the production of 'collective effervescence.'
4. On Adler and the founding of Ethical Culture, see Horace L. Friess, Felix Adler and Ethical Culture (Columbia University Press, 1981), a sympathetic and historically careful account by one of Adler's successors.
