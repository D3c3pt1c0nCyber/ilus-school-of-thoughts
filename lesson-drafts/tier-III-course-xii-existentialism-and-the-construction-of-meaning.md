# Existentialism and the Construction of Meaning

*On Camus, Sartre, de Beauvoir, and Frankl, and whether a life can be made meaningful in an indifferent world.*

In the winter of 1942, while German troops occupied Paris and the printing of certain books required a censor's stamp, a young Algerian newspaperman published a short essay that opened with a sentence of startling composure: there is only one truly serious philosophical problem, and that is suicide. Albert Camus did not mean this provocatively. He meant that every other question — what to know, how to act, whether a god exists — depends upon a prior decision to remain alive in a world that has not asked our opinion. The lesson before us begins where he began: with that decision, and with what follows it.

## I. The Absurd, and the Refusal to Look Away

Camus's figure for the human condition was Sisyphus, condemned by the gods to roll a boulder up a hill for eternity. The image is older than philosophy, but Camus drew from it a strange and modern conclusion. The tragedy lies not in the labor but in the moment at the summit, when Sisyphus turns and watches the stone roll back down. That pause — the interval of lucid consciousness — is where meaning is either lost or made. The universe, Camus argued, is not hostile to our hunger for sense; it is merely silent. The absurd is the encounter between that silence and our asking.[^1]

What Camus refused was the leap. He admired Kierkegaard, and treated Christian responses to despair with a seriousness his secular contemporaries often did not. But to resolve the absurd by appeal to God, or to History, or to any consoling totality, struck him as a kind of philosophical suicide — an exchange of the difficult question for a comforting answer. One must imagine Sisyphus happy, he wrote, in the closing line that has since become almost too familiar to read. He meant: happy without the answer.

## II. Freedom, Facticity, and the Weight of Choosing

Jean-Paul Sartre, lecturing at the Club Maintenant in October 1945, attempted to defend existentialism against the charge that it was a doctrine of despair. The lecture, hastily transcribed and published the next year as Existentialism is a Humanism, contained the formulation that would travel furthest: existence precedes essence. There is no human nature given in advance, no blueprint to which we must conform; we are, first, and what we are is the residue of what we have done. This was meant as liberation, though Sartre acknowledged the vertigo. To be free, in his sense, is to be responsible for the self one is becoming, without recourse to alibi.

> Man is condemned to be free; condemned, because he did not create himself, yet nonetheless free, because once thrown into the world, he is responsible for everything he does.
>
> — Jean-Paul Sartre, Existentialism is a Humanism (1946)

Simone de Beauvoir, who shared Sartre's premises but not always his conclusions, pressed the matter further in The Ethics of Ambiguity (1947). Freedom, she argued, is not a private achievement but a relation — one is free only in a world of other freedoms, and to will one's own freedom is necessarily to will theirs. Her existentialism was less a doctrine of solitary authenticity than a moral phenomenology of situation: the body, the historical moment, the structures of oppression that constrain what choosing can mean. She remains, perhaps, the movement's most underread figure, and the one whose questions have aged least.

## III. Meaning Found, Meaning Made

Viktor Frankl wrote the first draft of what became Man's Search for Meaning in nine days, in 1945, shortly after his liberation from the camps. He had lost his wife, his parents, his brother, and the manuscript of an earlier book sewn into the lining of a coat taken from him at Auschwitz. What he carried out was a clinical conviction: that human beings can endure almost any how if they possess a why. Logotherapy, the school of psychotherapy he founded, treated meaning not as a metaphysical claim but as a psychological necessity — something discovered in particular tasks, particular loves, particular sufferings borne well.[^2]

Where Camus insisted that meaning must be made without illusion, and Sartre that it must be made without excuse, Frankl suggested that it is often found rather than fabricated — encountered in the specific call of one's situation. These are not the same view, and the lesson does not ask you to choose between them. What they share is a refusal to outsource the question. The universe, on each account, will not arrive bearing instructions. What remains is the matter we will take up in the next lesson: whether ethics, deprived of cosmic backing, can still command us — and on what authority it would do so.

∴ The stone, the summit, the silence — and the climb resumed.

## Notes

1. Albert Camus, The Myth of Sisyphus (Le Mythe de Sisyphe), Gallimard, 1942. The English translation by Justin O'Brien (Knopf, 1955) remains standard.
2. Viktor E. Frankl, Man's Search for Meaning, first published in German in 1946 as Ein Psychologe erlebt das Konzentrationslager. Frankl borrowed the formulation about the how and the why from Nietzsche, whom he cites directly.
3. Simone de Beauvoir, The Ethics of Ambiguity (Pour une morale de l'ambiguïté), 1947. The English translation by Bernard Frechtman appeared in 1948.
4. Jean-Paul Sartre, Existentialism is a Humanism (L'existentialisme est un humanisme), 1946, based on the lecture delivered 29 October 1945.
