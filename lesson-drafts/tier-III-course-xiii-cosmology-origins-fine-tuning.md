# Cosmology, Origins, and the Fine-Tuning Argument

*On what modern physics has, and has not, changed in the oldest argument we keep having.*

In the eleventh century, a Persian theologian named al-Ghazali set down a syllogism so compact it could be carried in a pocket: whatever begins to exist has a cause; the universe began to exist; therefore the universe has a cause. Nine hundred years later, William Lane Craig recovered the argument from the seminaries and pressed it into the seminar rooms, armed now with Edwin Hubble's redshifts and the Penrose-Hawking singularity theorems. The premises had not changed. The evidence had. What follows is an attempt to ask, calmly, what the new evidence actually weighs — and what, after all the cosmology, remains a matter of metaphysical taste.

## I. The Kalam, Revisited

Craig's reformulation of the kalam argument, set out at length in his 1979 monograph, depends on a deceptively simple second premise: that the universe began to exist. For most of intellectual history this was a theological claim. After 1929, when Hubble's measurements at Mount Wilson showed the recession of galaxies, it became a cosmological one. The standard hot Big Bang model — refined by Penzias and Wilson's accidental discovery of the cosmic microwave background in 1965 — gave the kalam a kind of empirical scaffolding it had never previously enjoyed. The universe, it seemed, had a birthday.

The trouble is that birthdays in physics are not what they are in liturgy. The singularity at t=0 is, as Penrose patiently explains in The Road to Reality, a breakdown of the theory rather than a description of an event. Whether something existed 'before' — a quantum vacuum, an inflating multiverse, a bouncing cosmos — is precisely what the equations refuse to settle. The premise survives. Its meaning has thinned.

## II. Six Numbers, and the Anthropic Reply

The fine-tuning argument moves the question from origins to parameters. Martin Rees, in Just Six Numbers, names the constants on which a life-permitting cosmos appears to balance: the strength of gravity relative to electromagnetism, the efficiency with which hydrogen fuses to helium, the density parameter omega, the cosmological constant lambda, the amplitude of primordial fluctuations, and the number of spatial dimensions. Adjust any of them by a small fraction and the universe becomes either a thin gas of hydrogen or a rapidly collapsing point — in neither case a place where carbon chemistry, much less curious primates, could arise.

> Our universe looks finely tuned because the alternative — a universe in which no one is alive to notice the tuning — is, by definition, never observed.
>
> — after Martin Rees, Just Six Numbers

The theist reads this as design. The physicist, increasingly, reads it as selection. If our universe is one bubble in an inflating multiverse, as Andrei Linde and others have proposed, then the constants vary across regions, and we should expect to find ourselves in one of the rare hospitable ones — for the same reason a fish expects to find itself in water. The anthropic principle is not a triumph; it is a deflation. It does not explain the dials. It explains why we are the ones reading them.

## III. What the Physics Has Not Settled

Sean Carroll, in The Big Picture, is careful about what naturalism is entitled to claim. The multiverse is not a confirmed object; it is a consequence of theories — eternal inflation, the string landscape — which themselves remain conjectural. The honest naturalist position is not that the multiverse has dissolved fine-tuning, but that fine-tuning no longer requires a designer in order to be intelligible. A brute fact, an unexplained constant, a selection effect across a wider ensemble: each is, philosophically, no more extravagant than positing an unembodied mind whose preferences happen to coincide with our biochemistry.

We will return, in the next lesson, to the argument from design as it was pressed by Paley and dismantled by Hume — a dismantling that predates Darwin, and which I think is still under-read. For now it suffices to notice this: the kalam has been sharpened by physics, and so has the reply to it. Neither side leaves the seminar room with a proof. Both leave with better questions than they brought.

∴ The dials are real. Who, or what, set them remains the older question.
